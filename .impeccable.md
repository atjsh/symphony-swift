## Design Context

### Users
Symphony is used by developer-operators who monitor and investigate autonomous coding-agent runs working against GitHub issues. They need to scan the system quickly for status, failures, and progress, then shift into deeper inspection when a run, session, or issue needs attention.

Their operating context is split between passive awareness and active investigation. The interface must support fast at-a-glance understanding without sacrificing the depth needed for forensic review of runs, logs, and issue state.

### Brand Personality
**Native. Restrained. Technical.**

The product should feel like an Apple developer tool rather than a branded dashboard. Its emotional goal is confidence through clarity: calm, precise, and trustworthy, with the tone of a well-designed instrument panel instead of a visually expressive product surface.

### Aesthetic Direction
Use the design system direction established in `~/git-atjsh/symphony-swift-progress-report` as the stylistic model for future work. The target feel is Apple Xcode-like: semantic system surfaces, native split-view and workspace patterns, restrained separators, subtle panel treatments, minimal accent usage, and dense but readable information design.

Support the active system appearance in both light and dark mode. Do not force dark mode or build around a custom branded palette. Prefer system colors, system typography, native controls, and platform conventions across both macOS and iOS, adapting patterns appropriately per platform instead of copying desktop chrome verbatim.

Avoid heavy branding, decorative gradients, ornamental illustrations, or any web-dashboard treatment that makes the app feel less like a first-party Apple developer utility. Use monospaced typography and stronger color only where they materially improve operational readability, status signaling, or data inspection.

### Design Principles
1. Native Apple developer-tooling first. Use Apple platform conventions, semantic surfaces, native controls, and workspace-style layouts before introducing custom styling.
2. System appearance over custom themes. Support both light and dark appearances through semantic system colors and materials rather than bespoke brand palettes or forced-dark assumptions.
3. Information clarity over decoration. Prioritize scanability, legibility, hierarchy, and operational trust ahead of visual flourish.
4. Cross-platform consistency through native adaptation. Keep one Apple-native design language across macOS and iOS while letting each platform express it through its own interaction patterns.
5. Functional emphasis only. Use accent color, status color, and monospaced typography only when they improve meaning, readability, or inspection speed.
